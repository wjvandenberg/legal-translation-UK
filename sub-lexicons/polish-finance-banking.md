# Polish — Finance and Banking Sub-Lexicon

> **Scope.** This sub-lexicon covers two closely-related domains in a single per-language file: **Finance & Banking** (primary) and **Trading & Capital Markets**. The two were merged here because their vocabulary overlaps heavily (debt instruments, securities, exchange regulation) and translators typically need both at once. The two cross-language English-reference lexicons remain split: see `references/finance-banking.md` and `references/trading-capital-markets.md` as separate files.

## Notes

- **"Kredyt" vs "pożyczka"**: Polish law distinguishes bank credit (kredyt — governed by
  Prawo bankowe, only banks may grant) from civil-law loan (pożyczka — k.c. art. 720 et seq.,
  may be granted by anyone). Translate accordingly: "credit agreement" for kredyt, "loan" for
  pożyczka. A "syndicated loan" granted by banks is still "umowa kredytu konsorcjalnego".
- **Odsetki maksymalne (maximum interest)**: k.c. art. 359 § 2¹ caps contractual interest at
  twice the statutory interest rate (stopa ustawowa). k.c. art. 481 § 2¹ caps default
  interest at twice the statutory default interest rate. Clauses in excess are reduced by
  operation of law. Flag when translating. Current caps are published by the Minister of
  Finance.
- **"Zastaw rejestrowy" (registered pledge)**: Governed by the Act on Registered Pledge and
  the Pledge Register of 6 December 1996. Key features: (1) registration in the pledge
  register (rejestr zastawów) is constitutive; (2) pledgor may retain possession; (3)
  out-of-court enforcement available if contractually agreed. The preferred security for
  movable assets in Polish finance practice.
- **"Hipoteka"**: Governed by the Act on Land and Mortgage Registers and Mortgage (1982).
  Since 2011, only one type of mortgage exists (hipoteka umowna) — the distinction between
  hipoteka zwykła and hipoteka kaucyjna was abolished. A single mortgage can secure
  multiple claims. Entry in the land and mortgage register (księga wieczysta) is
  constitutive.
- **"777 k.p.c." declaration**: A notarial declaration of voluntary submission to
  enforcement (oświadczenie o poddaniu się egzekucji) under art. 777 § 1 pkt 4–6 of the
  Code of Civil Procedure. Allows the creditor to obtain an enforcement title without
  court proceedings. Standard security device in Polish finance practice. Translate as
  "art. 777 declaration" or "voluntary submission to enforcement" with a translator's note.
- **WIBOR transition**: Poland is transitioning from WIBOR to WIRON (Warsaw Interest Rate
  Overnight) as the primary PLN reference rate. Legacy loans reference WIBOR; new loans
  increasingly reference WIRON. Flag in reference-rate clauses.
- **Zastaw finansowy (financial collateral)**: Under the Act on Certain Financial Collateral
  of 2 April 2004 (implementing Directive 2002/47/EC). Applies to financial instruments
  and cash among regulated institutions. Features appropriation remedies and close-out
  netting protections.
- **Banking licence and outsourcing**: Outsourcing of banking activities is regulated by
  arts. 6a–6d of Prawo bankowe. Outsourcing to non-EU service providers requires KNF
  consent. Flag in services contracts where a Polish bank is the client.
- **MPP (split payment)**: Split payment mechanism applies mandatorily to B2B invoices
  over PLN 15,000 for listed sensitive goods/services. The VAT portion is paid into a
  dedicated VAT account. Impacts payment clauses in commercial contracts.

## Notes

- **KNF supervision**: The KNF has wide-ranging powers over the capital market —
  authorisation of investment firms, approval of prospectuses, supervision of market
  operators, imposition of sanctions. Appeal lies to the administrative courts
  (WSA Warszawa, NSA). Reference as "Polish Financial Supervision Authority" on first
  mention.
- **Prospectus Regulation**: EU Regulation 2017/1129 applies directly. Polish national
  law (Public Offering Act) supplements with procedural rules on prospectus approval,
  publication, liability. Recent reforms increased thresholds for prospectus obligations
  (EUR 2.5m / 5m / 8m depending on context). Verify current figures.
- **MAR and closed periods**: PDMRs (osoby pełniące obowiązki zarządcze) are barred from
  dealing in the issuer's shares during a 30-day closed period before publication of
  financial reports. Transactions ≥ EUR 5,000 per calendar year require notification to
  KNF and the issuer within 3 business days.
- **Takeover regime**: Polish law requires a mandatory tender offer when acquiring
  control. Thresholds were revised in 2022 (single 50% threshold for mandatory bid
  replacing the former 33% and 66% tiers). The minimum price rules (art. 79 u.o.p.)
  require careful calculation (6-month and 24-month reference periods; highest acquirer
  price). Critical in M&A translations for listed targets.
- **Squeeze-out / sell-out**: Art. 82–83 u.o.p. grant shareholders holding at least 95%
  (in aggregate with a concert party) the right to squeeze-out minority; minority holders
  have a corresponding sell-out right. Price determined under art. 79 rules. Separate
  from Commercial Companies Code squeeze-out under k.s.h. art. 418 (for non-listed S.A.).
- **NewConnect and Catalyst**: Alternative trading systems (MTFs) operated by the
  Warsaw Stock Exchange. NewConnect hosts growth/smaller companies; Catalyst is the
  bond MTF. Lighter prospectus regime (information document) applies. Separate
  continuing obligations regime.
- **Dematerialisation**: Polish shares of listed companies and certain non-listed
  companies (S.A. and P.S.A. since 2021) are fully dematerialised — held on securities
  accounts with depositary participants. Paper share certificates are no longer
  recognised. Register of shareholders (P.S.A. / S.A.) replaced by securities-account
  records.
- **KDPW and KDPW_CCP**: KDPW is the Polish central securities depository (CSDR-authorised);
  KDPW_CCP is the Polish central counterparty (EMIR-authorised). Handle settlement and
  clearing of all exchange-traded transactions in Polish securities. Cross-border
  connections via TARGET2-Securities.
- **EMIR and MiFID II — Polish implementation**: Both regimes apply in Poland through
  direct EU regulation or transposition via u.o.i.f. amendments. KNF is the competent
  authority. Reporting goes to authorised trade repositories (TRs). EMIR REFIT (applying
  since April 2024) has reshaped reporting formats.
- **Obligacje (bonds) regime**: Separate Bond Act of 15 January 2015. Regulates issuance
  of all corporate bonds (admitted or not admitted to trading). Introduces mandatory
  registration of bonds in KDPW (or bond registry for non-admitted) to improve
  transparency after the 2018 GetBack crisis.
- **AIFM authorisation**: ZAFI (AIFM) authorisation is required for managing AIFs
  (including closed-end FIZ and dedicated structures). Sub-threshold AIFMs may operate
  under a lighter registration regime. Cross-border marketing of AIFs requires passport
  procedures.
- **Civil liability for prospectus**: Art. 22 u.o.p. establishes liability of the issuer,
  offeror, and others responsible for prospectus information. Burden of proof: claimant
  must show loss; respondents bear burden on absence of fault. 3-year limitation from
  public knowledge of the facts giving rise to liability.
- **BFG resolution regime**: Complements banking supervision for investment firms that
  hold client assets. Separate deposit-guarantee scheme for client cash held at domestic
  investment firms. See also `polish-finance-banking.md`.
- **SFDR / Taxonomy**: EU sustainable finance disclosure regulation and taxonomy apply
  directly. Polish asset managers and investment firms have substantial ongoing
  classification and disclosure workstreams. Flag product categorisation (Article 6 / 8
  / 9) carefully in translations.
