## Notes

- **"Marque" spelling**: Use "trade mark" (two words, UK) or "trademark" (one word, US)
  depending on target audience. Default to UK (two words).
- **"Droit d'auteur" vs "copyright"**: French droit d'auteur includes moral rights
  (attribution, integrity) that are perpetual and non-transferable. Common law copyright is
  primarily economic. Translate as "copyright" but flag the moral rights distinction.
- **"Saisie-contrefaçon"**: A specifically French ex parte procedure allowing the IP
  holder to obtain evidence of infringement by court-ordered seizure. No direct common
  law equivalent. Translate as "seizure for infringement" or "search and seizure order".
- **"Concurrence déloyale" and "parasitisme"**: French unfair competition concepts.
  "Parasitisme" specifically covers free-riding on another's reputation or investment
  without direct competition. No exact English equivalent — translate as "free-riding"
  or "misappropriation" with context.
- **INPI**: Institut National de la Propriété Industrielle — the French patent and
  trade mark office. Keep the abbreviation "INPI" and translate on first mention.

## Notes

- **"Licence"**: French uses "licence" for both noun and verb. UK English uses
  "licence" (noun) / "license" (verb). US English uses "license" for both. Default
  to UK noun form.
- **"Infogérance"**: A specifically French term for IT outsourcing / managed services.
  No single English equivalent — translate based on the specific services being provided.
- **"Recette"**: In French IT contracts, "recette" refers to acceptance testing (UAT).
  Translate as "acceptance testing" or "user acceptance testing", not "recipe".
- **"Réversibilité"**: The process of migrating data and services back to the client
  or a new provider at end of contract. Translate as "exit" or "disengagement" —
  standard in IT outsourcing contexts.
