## Calendar and Era Conventions — MANDATORY

Per the universal rule in `references/general-legal.md`, all dates in the
English translation must be in full Gregorian form. This subsection
records the Japanese-specific era table, conversion arithmetic, and
placeholder handling. **Never use `Reiwa`, `Heisei`, `Showa`, `Taisho`,
or `Meiji` (or their CJK forms 令和/平成/昭和/大正/明治) in the English
output, not even parenthetically.**

### Era table

| Era  | Period                     | Romanisation | First-year Gregorian |
|------|----------------------------|--------------|----------------------|
| 令和 | 1 May 2019 – present       | Reiwa        | 2019                 |
| 平成 | 8 Jan 1989 – 30 Apr 2019   | Heisei       | 1989                 |
| 昭和 | 25 Dec 1926 – 7 Jan 1989   | Showa        | 1926                 |
| 大正 | 30 Jul 1912 – 25 Dec 1926  | Taisho       | 1912                 |
| 明治 | 23 Oct 1868 – 30 Jul 1912  | Meiji        | 1868                 |

### Conversion examples

| Source | Output |
|---|---|
| `平成29年11月29日` | `29 November 2017` |
| `令和5年4月1日` | `1 April 2023` |
| `昭和64年1月7日` | `7 January 1989` (Showa 64 = Heisei 1 = 1989) |
| `平成元年` | `1989` (`元` = first year of era) |
| `令和10年` | `2028` (Gregorian = first-year + (era year − 1)) |

### Placeholders (unfilled date cells)

Drafts often contain blanks like `令和〇年〇月〇日` for a date to be filled
in later. Render the whole span as `[__] [Month] 20__` or
`[date to be inserted]` — do NOT translate the era segment as `Reiwa` and
do NOT apply the placeholder-zero rule to the year cell in
isolation. The era + placeholder-digit combination is a single semantic
unit; rendering it as `Reiwa 0` reads as "year zero" and is wrong on two
counts (era name preserved + meaningless year).

### Pre-Meiji dates

Rare in modern legal drafting. If encountered, render the Gregorian
equivalent (or a date range if the original era is ambiguous) and drop
the era reference. Add a brief Gregorian-bracketed note only if the
specific era is itself the subject of the legal reference (e.g., "the
[year] reform" where the era is named in the surrounding text).

## Notes

- **Japanese numbering convention**: Japanese legal texts use 条 (article), 項 (paragraph),
  号 (item). A reference like "第5条第2項第3号" means Article 5, Paragraph 2, Item 3.
  Preserve this hierarchical structure.
- **"甲" / "乙"**: Japanese contracts traditionally designate parties as 甲 (Party A) and
  乙 (Party B). Translate as "Party A" / "Party B" or replace with the actual party names
  where identified.
- **Civil Code (民法)**: Japan's Civil Code was substantially reformed in 2020 (債権法改正).
  Key changes include the new warranty regime for contracts (契約不適合責任), replacing the
  prior "defect warranty" (瑕疵担保責任). Flag pre-reform vs post-reform terminology.
- **"信義則" (principle of good faith)**: A foundational principle in Japanese civil law
  (Art. 1(2) Civil Code). Courts apply it broadly. Translate as "principle of good faith"
  or simply "good faith" depending on context.
- **"法人"**: In Japanese law, legal persons include 株式会社 (kabushiki kaisha / KK),
  合同会社 (godo kaisha / GK), and various public interest legal persons. Translate as
  "legal person" or "juridical person" and specify the entity type where relevant.
