## Notes

- **Polish Civil Code (k.c.)**: The Civil Code of 23 April 1964 remains the core codification
  of Polish civil law, extensively amended since 1990 to accommodate the market economy and
  EU law. Contract law is found in Book III (Zobowiązania — Obligations).
- **Polish citation convention**: Polish legislation uses art. (artykuł) followed by § for
  subsections where applicable, then ust. (ustęp) and pkt (punkt). A reference like
  "art. 483 § 1 k.c." means Article 483, § 1 of the Civil Code. Preserve this structure.
- **"Zasady współżycia społecznego"**: Literally "principles of social coexistence" — a
  general clause in Polish civil law (art. 5 k.c.) functioning similarly to good faith and
  fair dealing in other civil-law systems. The closest English equivalent is "good faith and
  fair dealing" but the concept is broader. Flag on first mention and consider a translator's
  note.
- **EU member state**: Poland is an EU member. EU regulations apply directly. EU directives
  are transposed through national legislation. Check for EU law dimensions, particularly in
  data protection, competition, consumer, and financial services matters.
- **"Przedawnienie"**: The general limitation period under k.c. art. 118 is 6 years for most
  civil claims and 3 years for claims arising from business activity. Shorter periods apply
  to specific claims. Since 9 July 2018, limitation is applied ex officio by the court in
  consumer cases.
- **"Nieważność"** (invalidity) vs **"unieważnienie"** (annulment): Polish law distinguishes
  absolute nullity (nieważność bezwzględna — void from inception) from relative nullity
  (nieważność względna — voidable on challenge). Render accurately as the distinction
  affects available remedies.
