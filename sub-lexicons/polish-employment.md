## Notes

- **Kodeks pracy (k.p.)**: The Labour Code of 26 June 1974 — Poland's comprehensive
  employment-law codification, extensively amended. Key provisions: art. 22 (definition
  of employment relationship), arts. 29–30 (contract form), arts. 32–52 (termination),
  arts. 128–151 (working time), arts. 154–173 (annual leave), arts. 18³ᵃ–18³ᵉ
  (non-discrimination), art. 94³ (mobbing).
- **"Umowa o pracę" vs civil-law engagements**: Polish law strictly distinguishes
  employment (umowa o pracę) from civil-law engagements (umowa zlecenia, umowa o dzieło,
  B2B). If the actual performance bears the hallmarks of employment (subordination, fixed
  place and time, personal performance — art. 22 k.p.), the relationship is treated as
  employment regardless of label. Courts and PIP can reclassify. Flag this risk in
  advisory translations.
- **Fixed-term contract limits**: Under art. 25¹ k.p., the maximum total duration of
  fixed-term contracts with the same employer is 33 months and the maximum number is 3.
  A 4th contract or any period beyond 33 months is automatically treated as open-ended.
  Exceptions: replacement contracts, seasonal work, term connected to an objective reason.
- **Notice periods (art. 36 k.p.)**: Open-ended and fixed-term contracts: 2 weeks (<6
  months' employment), 1 month (6 months–3 years), 3 months (>3 years). Trial-period
  contracts have shorter notice (3 days to 2 weeks). Notice must be in writing with
  justification for open-ended contracts.
- **"Ciężkie naruszenie" (gross breach) — art. 52 k.p.**: Summary dismissal requires a
  specific, written statement of cause, served within 1 month of the employer becoming
  aware of the cause. Consultation with trade union (if applicable) required. Challenging
  party may claim reinstatement or compensation. Strictly limited grounds — flag
  consistently.
- **Post-termination non-compete (arts. 101¹–101⁴ k.p.)**: Must be in writing, specify
  duration, and provide compensation of at least 25% of salary the employee received
  pre-termination over the relevant period. Without compensation, the clause is
  unenforceable against the employee. The employer may terminate early only if
  contractually agreed.
- **Collective redundancies**: Governed by the Act of 13 March 2003. Applies when
  dismissals reach thresholds (10/10%/30 depending on workforce size) within 30 days.
  Triggers consultation, notification to PUP (labour office), and entitlement to
  severance pay (1–3 months' salary depending on tenure).
- **PPK (Pracownicze Plany Kapitałowe)**: Mandatory universal long-term savings scheme
  introduced 2019. All employers must offer PPK to employees unless a qualifying PPE is
  in place. Employee contributions default-on; employees may opt out.
- **Employee data**: Scope of employee personal data the employer may request is
  exhaustively listed in art. 22¹ k.p. Processing beyond the list requires employee
  consent or another GDPR basis. Flag in employment-related DPAs.
- **Praca zdalna (remote work)**: Formalised in k.p. arts. 67¹⁸–67³⁴ (effective April
  2023), replacing the older telework provisions. Includes cost reimbursement
  obligations, occupational safety duties, and rights to recall to the workplace.
- **B2B / self-employment arrangements**: Common in IT and professional services. Risk
  of ZUS or tax reclassification if the arrangement has employment hallmarks. Civil-law
  agreements with former employers within 2 years of termination may be scrutinised.
- **ZFŚS (Company Social Benefits Fund)**: Mandatory for employers with 50+ employees
  (as of 1 January; smaller employers may opt in). Benefits distribution must observe
  the "social criterion" (kryterium socjalne) — i.e., linked to the beneficiary's family
  and financial situation.
