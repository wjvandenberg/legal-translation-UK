# German — Corporate / M&A / JV / Resolutions / POA Sub-Lexicon

Unified German-to-English mapping covering M&A documents, joint ventures,
shareholders' agreements, shareholder loans, corporate resolutions and corporate
governance (including the German two-tier board structure), and powers of attorney
/ Vollmacht / Prokura / proxies.

For substantive English vocabulary and drafting notes, see
`references/corporate-ma-jv.md`.

## Notes

- **"Completion" vs "Closing"**: UK practice uses "completion"; US practice uses "closing".
  Default to UK unless the document context or governing law indicates US style.

- **"Gesellschafterdarlehen"**: Under German insolvency law (§ 39 InsO), shareholder loans
  are automatically subordinated in insolvency. This is different from contractual
  subordination. Flag in translation if relevant.

- **"Geschäftsanteile" (GmbH) vs "Aktien" (AG)**: "Geschäftsanteile" are quotas/interests
  in a GmbH; "Aktien" are shares in an AG. Both translate as "shares" in English but the
  distinction may be legally relevant.

- **"Vinkulierung"**: A transfer restriction requiring the company's consent before shares
  can be transferred. Translate as "consent clause" or "transfer restriction".

- **Notarial requirement**: Under German law, the transfer of GmbH shares requires
  notarial form (§ 15 GmbHG). Flag this requirement in translation if relevant.

- **"Gemeinschaftsunternehmen"**: The standard German term for joint venture. Also
  commonly referred to as "Joint Venture" (borrowed English).

- **"Zustimmungsvorbehalte"**: Decisions requiring the consent of all or specific
  shareholders. Translate as "reserved matters" (UK standard).

- **"Nachschusspflicht"**: An obligation to make additional contributions to the company
  if called upon (§ 26 ff. GmbHG). Translate as "obligation to make additional
  contributions" or "capital call obligation".

- **Dual board structure (AG)**: German AGs have a two-tier board: the Vorstand
  (management board — runs the company) and the Aufsichtsrat (supervisory board —
  oversees the Vorstand). NEVER confuse the two. The Vorstand is NOT the "board of
  directors" — that term combines both functions.

- **"Entlastung"**: A specifically German concept where the shareholders formally approve
  (discharge) the management's conduct for a past fiscal year. This does not exist in
  common law. Translate as "discharge" or "ratification of directors' actions" with note.

- **"Gesellschafterversammlung" vs "Hauptversammlung"**: The former is the meeting of
  GmbH shareholders; the latter is the meeting of AG shareholders. Both translate as
  "shareholders' meeting" but the governance rules differ significantly.

- **GmbH & Co. KG**: A very common German structure — a limited partnership where the
  general partner is a GmbH (limiting the general partner's liability). Preserve the
  structure designation.

- **"Vollmacht" vs "Prokura" vs "Handlungsvollmacht"**: German distinguishes general
  representation authority (Vollmacht) from statutory commercial powers. **Prokura**
  (§§ 48-53 HGB) is registered in the commercial register and gives the holder very
  broad authority for all commercial transactions of any business; do not translate as
  "power of attorney" alone — use "commercial power of attorney (Prokura)".
  **Handlungsvollmacht** (§§ 54 ff. HGB) is narrower — limited to acts customary in the
  particular business — translate as "commercial agency authority". Plain **Vollmacht**
  is the civil-law power of attorney — translate as "power of attorney".

- **"Einzelprokura" vs "Gesamtprokura"**: Sole vs joint Prokura. Translate as "sole
  Prokura" / "joint Prokura" and add an explanatory note where needed.

- **"Vorsorgevollmacht"**: A health-care/incapacity-planning POA. Translate as
  "enduring power of attorney" (UK) or "lasting power of attorney" (England/Wales after
  2007). Do not translate as "advance directive" — that is a different instrument.

- **"Stimmrechtsvollmacht"**: Translate as "voting proxy" (NOT "voting power of
  attorney"). The function — attendance and voting at a meeting — dictates the English
  term.

- **"Notarielle Beurkundung" vs "Notarielle Beglaubigung"**: The former is full notarial
  recording of the transaction; the latter is mere certification that a signature is
  genuine. Distinguish these — they have different legal effects.

- **Apostille vs legalisation**: Use "apostille" only for Hague Convention countries;
  use "consular legalisation" for non-Hague countries.
