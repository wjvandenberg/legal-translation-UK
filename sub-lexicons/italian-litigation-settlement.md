# Litigation & Settlement — Italian Sub-Lexicon

Italian→English mappings for contentious legal work: civil litigation, administrative
litigation, criminal proceedings, settlement agreements, releases, waivers, consent
orders, dispute-resolution clauses, mediation and arbitration agreements.

This sub-lexicon covers both (a) the full civil, administrative and criminal litigation
life-cycle (atti → udienza → sentenza → appello → esecuzione) and (b) the transactional
instruments used to resolve disputes without final judgment (accordi transattivi,
liberatorie, verbali di conciliazione, accordi omologati). The settlement terminology
section below (legacy) is preserved in full; the added civil/administrative/criminal
sections extend the sub-lexicon to cover live proceedings.

## Notes for Translators

- **"Transazione" context**: In Italian legal documents, "transazione" appears in both dispute
  resolution and commercial contexts. Always determine from context whether "settlement"
  (dispute resolution) or "transaction" (commercial) is intended. Dispute resolution contexts
  require "settlement".

- **"Reciproche concessioni"**: Articles 1965 Italian Civil Code require "reciproche concessioni"
  (mutual concessions) for valid settlement. This is a validity requirement under Italian law
  but not under English law. The concept should be accurately translated but not glossed with
  common law equivalents.

- **"Tombale"**: Italian metaphor (literally "tombstone") for a settlement that is full and
  final, barring any further claims. Translate as "full and final settlement" or "complete
  release" — the metaphor is not used in English legal English.

- **Consent orders**: Italian "accordo omologato" and "verbale di conciliazione" filed with
  court are the Italian equivalents of English "consent order". The Italian procedure differs
  from English Tomlin orders but the functional equivalent is "court-approved settlement".

- **"Saldo e stralcio"**: Idiomatic Italian phrase meaning complete and final settlement
  (literally "balance and strike off"). Translate as "full and final settlement" or "complete
  settlement".

- **Italian vs common law settlement**: Settlements under Italian law have different validity
  requirements and effects from English law settlements. Do not add common law glosses or
  equivalences — translate the Italian terms accurately.

## Notes for Translators — Litigation

- **"Sentenza" is NOT "sentence" (civil/administrative)**: In civil and administrative contexts, "sentenza" is always "judgment" in English. "Sentence" is reserved for criminal proceedings (the punishment imposed on conviction). This is one of the most common and most damaging calques in Italian→English legal translation.

- **"Imputato" is NOT "imputed"**: The correct English equivalents are "defendant" or "accused". Never calque.

- **"Interesse legittimo"**: A core concept of Italian administrative law with no direct common-law equivalent. It occupies the space between a subjective right (diritto soggettivo) and a mere factual interest. Retain the Italian term with a translator's note where precision is required, or use "legitimate interest" with a note explaining the civil-law distinction.

- **"Eccesso di potere"**: Italian umbrella ground of administrative review that covers what in English law would be split between irrationality (Wednesbury) and procedural impropriety. Translate as "excess of power" or "irrationality/procedural impropriety" depending on the facts; a translator's note helps.

- **"Pubblico ministero"**: Never calque as "public ministry". Correct: "public prosecutor" or "prosecuting authority". In Italian documents the acronym "PM" is standard — may be retained with a translator's gloss on first use.

- **"D.Lgs. 231/2001"**: The Italian corporate quasi-criminal liability regime. Technically classified as "administrative" under Italian law but is functionally criminal (investigated by prosecutors, tried in criminal courts, involves fines and disqualifications). In English, "corporate (quasi-)criminal liability" or "administrative liability of entities" with a translator's note is the safest formulation.

- **"Patteggiamento"**: Italian plea-bargaining procedure. "Plea bargaining" or "plea agreement" is acceptable, but note that the Italian procedure has specific features (e.g. the court reviews the agreement and issues a special form of sentence). Translator's note helpful in technical contexts.

- **"Parte civile"**: The Italian mechanism by which a victim joins criminal proceedings to claim damages. There is no direct common-law equivalent (UK/US victims bring separate civil proceedings). Retain or use "civil party" with a translator's note.

- **"Giudicato"**: "Res judicata" is the direct translation, but "giudicato" can also mean "final judgment" (the judgment that has become res judicata). Context dictates — sometimes "final and binding judgment" reads better.

- **"Esecuzione" vs "sentenza esecutiva"**: "Esecuzione" = enforcement/execution. "Esecutivo" = enforceable. Do not calque "sentenza esecutiva" as "executive judgment" — correct is "enforceable judgment".

- **"Cassazione"**: Keep the capitalised English name "Court of Cassation" for the Corte di Cassazione. The concept of cassation (appeal on points of law only, without retrial of facts) is civil-law-specific — a translator's note is warranted where the procedural limits matter.
