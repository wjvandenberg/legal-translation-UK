## Notes

- **"Pénalités"**: In French law, contractual penalties (clause pénale) can be adjusted
  by the court if disproportionate. Always translate as "liquidated damages" (LDs), never
  as "penalties" — the English common law distinction matters.
- **"Responsabilité décennale"**: A mandatory 10-year liability for structural defects
  under French law (Art. 1792 Code civil). No common law equivalent. Translate as
  "decennial liability" with a context note.
- **"Réception"**: The formal process of acceptance of works. "Réception provisoire" is
  provisional acceptance (allowing the employer to take over); "réception définitive" is
  final acceptance after the defects liability period.
- **"Maître d'ouvrage" vs "maître d'œuvre"**: The maître d'ouvrage is the employer/owner;
  the maître d'œuvre is the project manager or engineer who supervises on behalf of the
  employer. Never confuse the two.

- **"Complément de rémunération"**: The French feed-in premium mechanism introduced
  by the 2015 Energy Transition Act. Similar to a contract for difference (CfD).
  Translate as "feed-in premium" or "contract for difference" depending on context.
- **"Délégation de service public" (DSP)**: A specific French public law mechanism
  for delegating operation of a public service to a private operator. Broader than
  a simple concession. Translate as "public service delegation" with context note.
- **"Biens de retour" vs "biens de reprise"**: "Biens de retour" automatically revert
  to the public authority at the end of the concession; "biens de reprise" may be
  repurchased at the authority's option. These are distinct legal concepts under
  French administrative law.
- **CRE**: The Commission de régulation de l'énergie is the French energy regulator.
  Keep the French abbreviation "CRE" and provide the English translation on first mention.
