# Italian Sub-Lexicon: Employment / Labour

Italian-to-English mappings extracted from employment.md. These terms are Italy-specific
and should be mapped to universal English equivalents in the main lexicon rather than
appearing in employment contracts written for English readers.

|---|---|---|

## Italian Employment Categories (statutory classifications)

| Italian term | English equivalent | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| dirigente | executive / senior manager | Italian statutory employment category (not just job title); distinct rules for dismissal, notice (typically 1 month), severance, and benefits; requires significant decision-making authority; high salary; NOT entitled to TFR (instead TFR-equivalent paid as separate fund or to supplementary pension); equivalent to "C-suite" or senior management but more legally defined |
| quadro | middle manager | Italian statutory employment category; intermediate between "impiegato" and "dirigente"; decision-making role; sometimes called "quadri" (plural); dismissal and notice rules distinct |
| impiegato | white-collar employee | Italian statutory employment category; office worker, professional, administrative role; may have sub-levels under CCNL (e.g., 1°, 2°, 3° level); entitled to TFR; standard dismissal/notice rules |
| operaio | blue-collar worker | Italian statutory employment category; manual/production worker; may have sub-levels under CCNL; entitled to TFR; standard dismissal/notice rules |

## Key Translation Notes

- **CCNL (Contratto Collettivo Nazionale di Lavoro)**: Master collective agreement governing entire sector/industry.
  Mandatory in Italian employment law. In English translation, retain CCNL abbreviation with full English name on
  first use (e.g., "CCNL Commercio, the National Collective Bargaining Agreement for the Retail Sector").
  Key content: employment categories, salary grades, working hours, leave entitlements, notice periods, TFR
  treatment, disciplinary procedures, etc.

- **"Giusta causa" vs "giustificato motivo"**: Critical distinction in Italian law with different legal consequences:
  * Giusta causa (Article 2119 Civil Code): serious misconduct; allows immediate dismissal without notice; employee
    not entitled to notice pay; less favorable severance
  * Giustificato motivo (Article 2119, Law 604/1966): lesser grounds; requires notice period; full severance/TFR;
    procedural requirements more relaxed
  Do NOT conflate or translate both as "dismissal for cause"; maintain Italian legal framework.

- **Employment categories** (dirigente, quadro, impiegato, operaio): Statutory Italian classifications under CCNL
  with distinct rules on notice, severance, dismissal, benefits. Not arbitrary job titles. In English translation:
  * Dirigente = executive/senior manager (distinct rules; shorter notice; no TFR paid directly; often higher
    compensation; typically 1-month notice)
  * Quadro = middle manager (intermediate status; enhanced notice/dismissal protection; entitled to TFR)
  * Impiegato = white-collar/professional (may have sub-levels 1°, 2°, 3° under CCNL; entitled to TFR)
  * Operaio = blue-collar/manual worker (may have sub-levels under CCNL; entitled to TFR)
  Note Italian category in sub-lexicon; translate to English-language description in main lexicon.

- **TFR (Trattamento di Fine Rapporto)**: Article 2120 Italian Civil Code. Statutory severance accruing annually
  (~1/13.5 monthly salary per year), paid upon termination (retirement, resignation, dismissal, death). NO exact
  common-law equivalent. Translate as "severance indemnity" or "end-of-service allowance" and retain "TFR" in
  parentheses on first use. Note: Dirigente may not receive TFR directly (instead TFR-equivalent paid to pension
  fund or separate arrangement).

- **"Comporto" (protected sick leave period)**: Italian law concept. Maximum cumulative period employer must tolerate
  before dismissal becomes lawful (typically 180 days per Article 2110 Civil Code, but determined by CCNL and case
  law; varies by sector and company agreement). Not same as "protected sick leave" in UK/US context.

- **Dismissal remedies**: Italian law provides two remedies for procedurally invalid/unjustified dismissals:
  * Tutela reale (reinstatement remedy): restoration to position or equivalent plus back pay
  * Tutela obbligatoria (compensation remedy): monetary compensation instead of reinstatement
  Both may apply depending on dismissal grounds and procedural violations. Do NOT use UK "unfair dismissal" standard
  unless source specifically maps to it.

- **"Somministrazione di lavoro"** (temporary agency work): Regulated by Articles 30 et seq. Legislative Decree
  276/2003 (Biagi reform) as amended by Legislative Decree 81/2015 (Jobs Act); earlier "lavoro interinale" was
  introduced by Law 196/1997 and has since been superseded. Distinct from secondment (distacco). Agency is the
  legal employer; client is only user of services. Subject to equal treatment rules (pay, benefits, working
  conditions vs direct employees of client).

- **"Distacco"** (secondment): Temporary assignment while retaining home employer. Regulated by Article 30,
  Legislative Decree 276/2003 (Biagi reform). If cross-border, EU Posted Workers Directive applies (96/71/EC,
  updated 2018/957). Different from "trasferta" (business travel).

- **Jobs Act references**: Italian employment law underwent major reform with Legislative Decree 23/2015 (Jobs Act).
  Key changes: eliminated co.co.co. as standard form; introduced "genuinely independent" criteria for contracts;
  modified dismissal remedies for smaller companies (different compensation caps); changed probationary rules;
  enhanced protection for permanent contracts. If source references pre- or post-Jobs Act law, note distinction in
  sub-lexicon.

- **Notice periods**: Vary by CCNL, employment level, and dismissal grounds. Examples:
  * Blue-collar ("operaio"): often 1-2 days notice
  * Office/professional ("impiegato"): typically 1-2 months
  * Middle management ("quadro"): typically 1-3 months
  * Executive ("dirigente"): typically 1 month (often shorter than impiegato)
  Always reference applicable CCNL or employment law for specific notice period.

- **CIG (Cassa Integrazione Guadagni)**: Italian wage supplementation fund. Government-sponsored program providing
  partial income (typically 80% of wage) to employees whose hours are reduced due to temporary economic/technical
  difficulties (CIG ordinaria) or major restructuring (CIG straordinaria). Worker remains employed; not same as
  unemployment benefits. Duration varies by type and government authorization.

- **Mobilità**: Italian redundancy scheme providing income support to workers displaced by restructuring. Distinct
  from CIG; signals likely permanent dismissal following restructuring.

- **INPS / INAIL**: Mandatory insurance schemes:
  * INPS (Istituto Nazionale della Previdenza Sociale): national social security institute; administers pensions,
    unemployment, maternity, disability benefits
  * INAIL (Istituto Nazionale per l'Assicurazione contro gli Infortuni sul Lavoro): workplace injury insurance;
    employer pays contribution; covers workplace accidents and occupational diseases

- **Collective redundancy procedure (procedura di licenziamento collettivo)**: Required when employer dismisses
  multiple employees due to restructuring. Mandatory consultation with unions, social plan negotiation, minimum
  notice (varies by numbers affected, typically 1-2 months). Failure to follow procedure may invalidate dismissals.
