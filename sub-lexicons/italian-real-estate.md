# Italian Real Estate Sub-Lexicon

Italian-specific terms and references for real estate. These entities, regulations, and cadastral/administrative systems are Italy-specific and should be translated to English in the main lexicon.

## Notes

- **Caparra concepts**: Italian law distinguishes two deposit types:
  - *Caparra confirmatoria* (article 1385 CC): Earnest money serving as both confirmation of agreement and liquidated damages (usually non-refundable if buyer withdraws).
  - *Caparra penitenziale* (article 1386 CC): Right-of-withdrawal deposit (either party can withdraw; the withdrawing party forfeits/repays the deposit).
  These civil law concepts have no exact common-law equivalents. In English translations, use the English descriptions; retain the Italian term in parentheses on first use.

- **Condominium (condominio)**: In Italian law, this refers to the co-ownership regime of a multi-unit building, not a specific building type. Governed by Title VI-bis, Book III, Italian Civil Code (articles 1100-1139). A condominium has mandatory common areas (stairs, roof, walls, grounds) and optional common areas. Each unit owner is liable for common charges proportionately (tabelle millesimali).

- **Nuda proprietà and usufrutto**: Unique civil law concept allowing split ownership: one party holds bare title (nuda proprietà), another has usufruct (right to use and receive income). Not present in common law. Always translate accurately and explain on first use.

- **Superficies**: Civil law right to construct on or below another's land. Rare in common law; UK has "fee farm rent"; US has "easement for building". Italian Code, article 952 et seq.

- **Easement (servitù)**: Italian law uses "servitù"; English uses "easement". Both refer to rights benefiting one parcel and burdening another (access, utilities, parking, light/view). Italian servitù are codified in articles 1027-1099 CC.

- **ISTAT indexation**: ISTAT (Istituto Nazionale di Statistica) publishes monthly inflation indices. Italian leases typically index rent to "FOI" (Indice dei prezzi al consumo per le famiglie di operai e impiegati = consumer price index for worker families) or other indices. Always specify which ISTAT index if mentioned.

- **Energy Performance Certificate (APE)**: Italian acronym is APE (Attestato di Prestazione Energetica). Required for all buildings in Italy since January 2008 (Law 90/2013, implementing EU Directive 2010/31/EU). Must be provided at lease signature or before property sale.

- **Cedolare secca**: Flat-rate tax regime (Law 431/1998, updated). Allows landlord/tenant to opt out of normal income tax and capital gains tax; standard rate approximately 21% on declared rent (residential) or 26% (commercial). Major simplification but locks parties into declared rent.

- **Cadastral references**: Italian property identified by catasto entries:
  - *Foglio*: cadastral sheet/map number
  - *Particella*: parcel number within sheet
  - *Subalterno*: sub-unit number (for buildings)
  For example: "Foglio 42, Particella 156, Subalterno 5" identifies a specific apartment. Always provide the English labels but retain the numerical references.

- **Building permits vs. notifications**: Italian law distinguishes:
  - *Permesso di costruire*: Full building permit for major works; requires detailed application and approval.
  - *SCIA* (Certified notification): Simplified procedure for minor works; notification-based rather than approval-based (Law 326/2003).
  - *DIA* (older procedure, largely superseded): Notification of commencement of works.

- **Destinazione d'uso**: This term can mean either (1) land zoning (urban planning designation) or (2) permitted use within a lease. Context is critical. If referring to zoning, translate as "zoning" or "planning designation"; if lease-related, translate as "permitted use".

- **Condominium regulations**: Article 1118, Italian Civil Code, allows condominiums to adopt regulations governing use of common areas. These are binding on all unit owners.

- **Apportionment tables (tabelle millesimali)**: Required for all condominiums. Each unit is assigned a share expressed in "millesimi" (thousandths) reflecting its proportion of common areas and corresponding liability for common charges (spese condominiali). These tables are mandatory and very important for calculating charges.

- **Italian notary (notaio)**: In Italian law, property transfers require a notarial deed. The notary is an independent public official (not a lawyer, though trained in law). Their role includes verifying identities, ensuring legal capacity, and maintaining records. This is mandatory for property transfers and some other transactions.

- **Lease protections**: Law 431/1998 reformed residential tenancy law, introducing fixed-term renewable leases, protections against eviction without cause, and allowing "cedolare secca" tax regime. Always check which lease regime applies (protected vs. free market).
