# French — Taxes Sub-Lexicon

French tax-law terminology mapped to the neutral English equivalents in
`references/taxes.md`. French tax law is governed primarily by the Code général
des impôts (CGI), the Livre des procédures fiscales (LPF), and the Bulletin
officiel des finances publiques – Impôts (BOFiP-Impôts), the administrative
doctrine. For corporate / M&A context use alongside `french-corporate-ma-jv.md`.

## 15. Notes — Translator Guidance

- **IS vs "corporate tax"**. Impôt sur les sociétés (standard rate 25%; reduced
  15% SME rate up to €42,500) is France's corporate income tax. Translate as
  "corporate income tax (IS)" on first mention — not "corporation tax" (UK-only)
  or "company tax" (informal).
- **Territoriality of IS.** France taxes corporate profits on a territorial basis
  (Art. 209 I CGI): a French company is subject to IS only on profits of
  enterprises operated in France (including French PEs). This is a significant
  deviation from the worldwide income principle prevailing elsewhere. Always flag
  when translating cross-border structures.
- **PAS (prélèvement à la source).** Since 2019, French personal income tax is
  collected via withholding at source by the employer / pension fund / bank
  (PAS). The PAS rate is personalised based on the prior year's return. Translate
  as "pay-as-you-earn (PAS)" and do not conflate with the WHT concept applicable
  to non-residents.
- **CSG / CRDS — social levies or tax?** The *contribution sociale généralisée*
  and *contribution pour le remboursement de la dette sociale* have mixed
  characterisation. Domestic law treats them as social contributions; the CJEU
  has characterised part of CSG as a tax for treaty purposes (*de Ruyter*, 2015).
  For treaty-access disputes and gross-up clauses, translate as "social levies /
  social contributions" with a translator's note on the mixed characterisation.
- **Abus de droit vs acte anormal de gestion.** *Abus de droit* (Art. L64 LPF) is
  the French GAAR — applies to schemes (i) fictitious or (ii) driven exclusively
  by tax motive that is contrary to the law's intent. *Acte anormal de gestion*
  is a different doctrine — a management act without adequate business
  justification (typically shifting tax base to related parties). The 2018 reform
  added the *mini abus de droit* for acts driven "principally" (not exclusively)
  by tax motive. Always translate precisely.
- **Rescrit fiscal.** France has a well-developed advance ruling system, issued
  by the DGFiP and published in the BOFiP-Impôts. Binding on the administration
  when the facts match. Translate as "tax ruling".
- **Prescription 3 / 6 / 10 years.** General reassessment period is 3 years
  (Art. L169 LPF). Extends to 6 years for omitted / understated income, 10 years
  for undisclosed foreign accounts and certain aggravated cases. Always translate
  as "prescription period" or "reassessment period" with the number of years, and
  identify Art. L169 LPF for the general rule.
- **Régime mère-fille.** French participation exemption on dividends: 95% of the
  dividend is exempt (i.e., 5% addback for expenses, Art. 216 CGI), subject to
  holding ≥5% for ≥2 years. Translate as "parent-subsidiary regime" or
  "participation exemption (Art. 216 CGI)".
- **Plus-values à long terme sur titres de participation.** Capital gains on
  qualifying shareholdings (≥5%, ≥2 years) are effectively exempt (subject to
  12% addback). Translate as "long-term capital gains regime on qualifying
  shareholdings" or "French participation exemption on capital gains".
- **Intégration fiscale.** Optional tax consolidation regime (Arts. 223 A et
  seq. CGI) requiring ≥95% ownership for the entire year. The group files one
  consolidated IS return. Translate as "tax consolidation regime".
- **IFI (Impôt sur la fortune immobilière).** In 2018, the broad-scope wealth
  tax (ISF) was replaced by the IFI, which is limited to real estate assets.
  Threshold: €1.3m net real estate wealth. Progressive rates 0.5%–1.5%. Translate
  as "real estate wealth tax (IFI)" and — when the source pre-dates 2018 —
  translate ISF as "wealth tax (ISF)" noting that it applied to all assets.
- **Pacte Dutreil.** Family-business transfer mechanism providing a 75% reduction
  in the base of inheritance / gift duties, subject to individual and collective
  holding commitments (6 years total). Translate as "Dutreil pact" with
  parenthetical explanation; do not translate the name.
- **Droits d'enregistrement on share transfers.** Transfers of shares in non-
  real-estate SAs are subject to 0.1% registration duty; transfers of shares in
  SARL or société civile at 3% (less €23,000 allowance); transfers of shares in
  real estate companies at 5% (on real estate value). Always identify the type
  of entity and asset profile.
- **CIR (crédit d'impôt recherche).** Major R&D tax credit (30% of qualifying
  expenditure up to €100m, 5% above). Translate as "research tax credit (CIR)"
  and add a translator's note on the rate structure when relevant.
- **BOFiP-Impôts.** Since 2012, the administrative tax doctrine is consolidated
  in the online BOFiP-Impôts (replacing the prior instructions and documentation
  de base). When a French source cites a BOI reference, preserve it as a citation
  and translate only the surrounding narrative.
- **Verrou de Bercy.** Before 2018, the tax administration had a near-monopoly
  on referring tax-fraud cases to the Parquet (criminal prosecution); the 2018
  fraud law broke this "lock" for significant cases (>€100k). Translate as
  "Bercy lock" or "prior-approval requirement" with a translator's note.
- **Pillar Two implementation.** France implemented Pillar Two via the Finance
  Law for 2024 (Art. 33). The *impôt complémentaire* has the three OECD
  modalities (IIR, UTPR, QDMTT). Translate as "top-up tax" and keep the OECD
  English acronyms.
- **Currency / number formats**. French uses space (or non-breaking space) as
  thousands separator and comma as decimal separator (1 234,56 €). Convert to UK
  English style unless the user specifies otherwise. Always preserve the euro
  sign and the exact figure.
