# French — Litigation & Settlement Sub-Lexicon

Maps French terms for contentious legal work — civil procedure, administrative procedure,
criminal procedure, settlement agreements, releases, waivers, consent orders, dispute
resolution clauses, mediation and arbitration — to their correct English equivalents.

This sub-lexicon covers both (a) the full civil, administrative and criminal litigation
life-cycle (assignation → audience → jugement → appel → cassation → exécution) and
(b) transactional instruments (transaction, protocole d'accord transactionnel,
renonciation, quittance, accord homologué). The settlement terminology below is preserved
in full; civil / administrative / criminal litigation sections extend the sub-lexicon
to live proceedings.

## Notes

- **"Transaction" (Art. 2044 Code civil)**: A settlement agreement under French law
  requires "concessions réciproques" (mutual concessions) to be valid. This is a
  validity requirement with no common law equivalent. Translate as "settlement agreement"
  but note the mutual concessions requirement if relevant.
- **"Exequatur"**: The French procedure for recognising and enforcing a foreign judgment
  or arbitral award. Translate as "recognition and enforcement" or "enforcement order".
- **"Référé"**: French summary proceedings allowing a judge to order interim measures
  quickly. Similar to common law interim/interlocutory applications. Translate as
  "summary proceedings" or "interim proceedings".
- **"Astreinte"**: A periodic penalty imposed by the court to compel performance.
  Similar to but distinct from contempt of court. Translate as "penalty payment" or
  "periodic penalty" with context note.
- **"Article 700 CPC"**: The French provision allowing the court to order one party to
  contribute to the other's legal fees. Not the same as a full costs order. Translate
  as "contribution to legal fees" or "costs order" with context note.

## Notes for Translators — Litigation

- **"Jugement" vs "arrêt"**: First-instance decisions are "jugements"; decisions of the Cour d'appel, Cour de cassation, and Conseil d'État are "arrêts". In English, both translate as "judgment", but a translator's note may be useful when the hierarchy matters.

- **"Sentence" is reserved for criminal contexts**: In civil and administrative contexts, "jugement" / "arrêt" = "judgment". "Peine" / "condamnation" in the criminal sense = "sentence". Do not calque.

- **"Partie civile"**: The French mechanism by which a victim joins criminal proceedings to claim damages. Retain the concept with "civil party" and add a translator's note — there is no direct common-law equivalent.

- **"Ministère public" / "parquet"**: Never calque as "public ministry". Correct: "public prosecutor's office" or "prosecuting authority". "Procureur" = "public prosecutor".

- **"Astreinte"**: A key French enforcement/compliance tool — daily penalty payments for non-performance. Retain "astreinte" (well known in international practice) or use "penalty payment" with a translator's note.

- **"Mis en examen"**: A French procedural status arising during "instruction" with no direct common-law equivalent. Translate as "person under formal investigation" or "charged person" depending on context, with a translator's note where precision matters.

- **"Dépens" vs "article 700"**: "Dépens" refers to statutory costs (limited categories). Article 700 of the CPC is a discretionary allowance for additional legal expenses (like solicitor's fees). Distinguish carefully in translation.

- **"Référé"**: French summary / urgent procedure heard by a single judge. Translate as "summary proceedings" or "urgent proceedings" and retain or identify the specific sub-type (référé-provision, référé-liberté, etc.) as needed.

- **"Fin de non-recevoir"**: A French procedural objection that bars the action without touching the merits (e.g. for lack of standing, limitation, res judicata). Translate as "bar to action" or "objection to admissibility" — not a "defence on the merits".

- **"Cour de cassation"**: Highest French civil/criminal court. Reviews only points of law. Keep English as "Court of Cassation". Cassation is civil-law-specific — translator's note warranted where the procedural limits matter.

- **"Conseil d'État"**: Highest French administrative court; also has advisory functions on draft legislation. "Council of State" is the standard English rendering.
