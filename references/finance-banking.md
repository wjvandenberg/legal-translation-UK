# Finance / Banking Lexicon

English terminology for finance and banking documents: project finance security packages, facility agreements, pledges, mortgages, bonds, loan agreements, intercreditor agreements, syndicated facilities, Basel III/CRR, AML/KYC, sanctions compliance, banking licences, and related instruments. Unified lexicon covering both project finance / security (formerly `finance.md`) and general banking / regulatory (formerly `banking-regulatory.md`).

> **Per-language sub-lexicon note.** This English reference file covers finance & banking only; trading & capital-markets terminology lives in `references/trading-capital-markets.md`. In the per-language sub-lexicons the two domains are consolidated: `sub-lexicons/<language>-finance-banking.md` covers both. When working on a capital-markets document, read both English reference files but only the single per-language sub-lexicon.

## English-Law Security Concepts

These terms are specific to English-law finance documents. Use them only when the source document's security structure genuinely maps to the English-law concept. Civil law security concepts often do not map neatly to common law categories.

| Correct English term | Usage / meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| debenture | A document creating fixed and floating charges over all or substantially all of a company's assets | Standard English-law security instrument in LMA deals |
| fixed charge | A charge attaching to specific, identified assets (the chargor cannot deal with them freely) | Maps well to civil law pledges over specific assets |
| floating charge | A charge hovering over a class of changing assets (e.g., inventory, receivables) until crystallisation | No direct civil law equivalent — use only where the source concept involves security over a fluctuating pool of assets |
| crystallisation | The moment a floating charge converts into a fixed charge over specific assets | — |
| security trustee | The entity holding security on trust for the benefit of all lenders (English-law PF structures) | Distinguished from "security agent" (which acts as agent, not trustee) |

## Notes

- **"Beneficiary" is ambiguous in security contexts**: The "beneficiary of the facility" is the borrower (who receives the money), but the "beneficiary of the security" is the secured party (who holds the security interest). Always disambiguate based on context. Never translate both as "borrower".

- **LMA standard**: English finance documents generally follow Loan Market Association (LMA) conventions. Align terminology with LMA standards where possible.

- **Loan vs. facility agreement**: Many source languages distinguish between a specific loan contract (bilateral, principal sum) and a broader credit facility. In English, use "facility agreement" (LMA standard) for syndicated/structured lending, and "loan agreement" for simple bilateral loans. Do not force source-language distinctions onto English; translate based on document structure and economic substance.

- **Project finance specifics**: In PF documentation, "Facility" always refers to a specific credit line (Term Facility, VAT Facility, DSR Facility). Never use "Loan" — "Facility" is the market standard.

- **"Article" vs "Clause"**: Use "Clause" for internal cross-references within the document. Keep "Article" only when referring to legislation.

- **Civil law vs common law security**: Many civil law security concepts (e.g., the Italian "privilegio speciale", the French "nantissement", the German "Sicherungsübereignung") do not map neatly to English-law categories. When translating, use the closest English-law term only if the concepts are genuinely equivalent. If not, use a descriptive English translation and consider a translator's note. Never force-fit a common law term onto a fundamentally different civil law concept.

- **National banking legislation**: When translating references to national banking codes or consolidated statutes, provide the official English translation on first mention followed by the original-language citation in parentheses. Sub-lexicon provides source-language mappings.

- **Capital requirements terminology**: Use exact Basel III/CRR terminology (CET1, AT1, T2, MREL, TLAC, SREP). These are internationally standardised. Do not paraphrase or translate abbreviations — they are used identically across jurisdictions.

- **EU regulatory framework**: EU directives/regulations (CRR, CRD, AMLD, PSD2) are multinational standards and retained in main English-only lexicon (not sub-lexicon). National-specific authorities and legislation go to sub-lexicon only.

- **AML directive versioning**: EU AML framework is evolving. Documents may reference AMLD4 (2015), AMLD5 (2018), AMLD6 (2022), or upcoming AML Regulation (AMLA). Always confirm which version applies and note in translation.

- **Bail-in and resolution**: "Bail-in" is EU-specific recovery/resolution tool under Banking Union framework (SRM/SSM). Distinct from US bankruptcy/receivership. "Resolution plan" / "living will" = required documentation of bank's failure strategy.

- **Passporting and ring-fencing**: "Passporting" = EU banking licence right (post-Brexit, applies within EU only, not UK). "Ring-fencing" = UK Vickers Report recommendation (implemented in UK, differs from EU structural separation proposals).

- **UK vs US English**: This lexicon uses UK conventions by default (e.g., "recognised", "licence" as noun, "organisation", "utilisation"). US alternatives noted where material (e.g., "authorization" vs "authorisation").
