# Transport and Insurance Lexicon

Standard English terminology for transport (maritime, road, rail, air, multimodal) and insurance agreements. Part 1 covers carriage contracts, bills of lading, charterparties, freight forwarding and transport liability regimes. Part 2 covers insurance contracts, life and non-life lines, reinsurance, brokers and claims. Marine insurance (Institute Cargo Clauses, hull and machinery, P&I), aviation insurance and carrier liability insurance sit at the intersection of both parts.

---

## Part 1 — Transport and Shipping

Covers carriage contracts, bills of lading, Incoterms, freight forwarding, maritime liens, carriage of goods, charterparties, logistics agreements and related instruments.

## Notes

- **BIMCO standard forms**: Shipping contracts often reference BIMCO standard forms
  (GENCON, NYPE, BALTIME, WORLDSCALE, etc.). Keep form names and acronyms in their
  original English form. Never translate.

- **Incoterms**: Always use the three-letter ICC Incoterms abbreviations (EXW, FOB, CIF, DAP,
  DDP, etc.) with full English names on first use. Incoterms® 2020 is the current edition
  (official as of January 2020). Never translate Incoterms abbreviations.

- **"Bill of lading" vs. "policy"**: Some source languages use a cognate term for both "bill of lading" (shipping) and "insurance policy". Always distinguish based on context: in shipping/carriage, use "bill of lading" (B/L); in insurance, use "policy". Sub-lexicon provides source-language mappings.

- **London arbitration**: Many charter parties provide for London arbitration under the
  LMAA (London Maritime Arbitrators Association) terms. Keep "LMAA" as-is; this is the
  standard English designation.

- **CMR Convention**: The Convention on the Contract for the International Carriage of
  Goods by Road (1956). Keep "CMR" and expand on first use as "CMR Convention" or
  "Convention on the Contract for the International Carriage of Goods by Road". CMR
  consignment note is the standard transport document.

- **CIM Convention**: International Convention concerning the Carriage of Goods by Rail.
  Keep "CIM" as-is; this is the standard English designation. CIM consignment note is
  the standard transport document.

- **Maritime English**: Maritime law has its own highly specialised English vocabulary
  developed over centuries of international practice. Use the established terms:
  - Use "demurrage" not "delay penalty"
  - Use "laytime" not "loading time" or "discharge time"
  - Use "dispatch" (or "despatch" in UK English) not "speed bonus"
  - Use "deviation" not "route change"
  - Use "charter party" not "chartering agreement"
  - Use "shipowner" not "ship owner" (though both are acceptable)
  These terms are standardized across all English-speaking maritime jurisdictions.

- **Bill of lading types**: Distinguish carefully between:
  - On-board B/L: signed after goods loaded on vessel (creates date certainty)
  - Received-for-shipment B/L: signed at port before loading (pre-loading)
  - Clean B/L: no exceptions to cargo condition
  - Claused B/L: notes defects or damage ("dirty" B/L in market parlance)
  Each type has distinct commercial/legal consequences.

- **Ship arrest vs attachment**: Maritime lien holders can arrest a vessel (seize by court
  order). General attachment is a broader civil law concept. Use "arrest of vessel" for
  maritime-specific seizure; use "attachment" only for non-maritime contexts.

- **Letter of indemnity (LOI)**: Issued to carrier when B/L cannot be produced (e.g., lost,
  delayed). LOI indemnifies carrier against liability for delivery without production of B/L.
  Standard maritime practice but creates off-balance-sheet risk.

- **Through bill of lading**: Single B/L covering multiple transport modes with single carrier
  to final destination. Distinct from "combined transport B/L" which may involve multiple
  carriers. Use "through B/L" when single carrier; "combined transport B/L" when multiple modes.

- **Hague-Visby / Hamburg / Rotterdam Rules**: International maritime conventions on B/L
  and carrier liability. Keep names as-is (e.g., "Hague-Visby Rules" not "Hague Visby").
  These are the three competing international regimes (Hague-Visby still most used; Rotterdam
  Rules most recent but limited adoption).

- **Safe port warranty**: Standard charter party warranty that named ports are safe for vessel.
  Extends beyond physical safety to legal safety (no war, embargo, etc.). Critical in
  charter party negotiations.

- **Classification society**: Examples include Lloyd's Register, DNV GL, American Bureau of
  Shipping (ABS), ClassNK. Keep society names as-is.

## Part 2 — Insurance and Reinsurance

## Core Insurance Terms

| Correct English term | Usage / meaning | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| insurance contract / insurance policy | Binding agreement between insurer and insured defining coverage, exclusions, conditions | policy agreement; coverage document (less precise) |
| policy | Contract of insurance; specify type (life, non-life, all-risks, multi-peril) | insurance agreement (less common usage) |
| life policy / life insurance policy | Insurance covering death, survival, annuities; may be term, whole life, or universal life | life insurance; mortality policy (too vague) |
| non-life policy / property and casualty policy (P&C) | Insurance against loss of property or liability; includes fire, theft, liability, marine | casualty policy; general insurance (regional) |
| all-risks policy / all-risks insurance | Coverage for all risks except those specifically excluded; more comprehensive than named-peril | blanket coverage; comprehensive insurance (imprecise) |
| multi-peril policy | Policy covering multiple named risks (e.g., fire AND theft AND water damage); distinct from all-risks | combination policy; combined coverage (vague) |
| named-peril policy | Coverage limited to explicitly listed perils only; narrower than all-risks | specified perils; enumerated coverage |
| general policy conditions / policy terms and conditions | Standard, non-negotiable provisions applying to all policies of that type; issued by insurer | standard terms; policy clauses (too vague) |
| special conditions / endorsements | Policy-specific amendments, rider clauses, or additional coverages modifying standard terms | riders; amendments (imprecise) |
| policy endorsement / rider / amendment | Document modifying, adding, or excluding coverage from the base policy | policy amendment; policy change (too vague) |
| certificate of insurance | Proof of insurance coverage issued by insurer or broker, not the actual policy | proof of insurance; insurance evidence (less precise) |

## Retention and Liability Limits

| Correct English term | Usage / meaning | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| excess (UK) / deductible (US) | Fixed amount insured must pay toward a claim; applies per claim or per occurrence | franchise (confuses with franchise deductible); retention (too vague) |
| absolute deductible / unconditional deductible | Deductible applies to ALL claims; insurer never pays the deductible amount | straight deductible; simple deductible |
| franchise deductible / franchise clause | Deductible applies only if loss exceeds threshold; if threshold exceeded, insurer pays full loss (no deductible) | franchise (alone); elimination period (confuses with disability insurance) |
| co-insurance / proportional retention | Insured retains fixed percentage of each claim (e.g., 10% co-insurance on claims); insurer pays remainder | shared loss; contributory retention (less standard) |
| compulsory retention / compulsory co-insurance | Contractual requirement that insured retain portion of risk; often imposed by reinsurers | mandatory co-insurance; forced retention (imprecise) |
| limit of indemnity (LoI) / sum insured | Maximum amount insurer will pay per claim or in aggregate | liability limit; coverage limit (too vague) |
| per-occurrence limit | Maximum insurer pays for any single loss event; resets annually | per-claim limit; single-incident limit (regional) |
| aggregate limit / annual aggregate | Maximum insurer pays across all claims in a policy period; typically annual or multi-year | total limit; combined limit (imprecise) |
| insured value / sum insured | Declared value of property or interest being insured; basis for premium calculation and limit | coverage amount; insurable value (less precise) |
| underinsurance / undervaluation | Situation where sum insured is less than actual value; may trigger average clause penalties | under-coverage; insufficient coverage (vague) |
| reinstatement (of sum insured) | Automatic restoration of limit to original amount after a claim; important for multi-claim exposures | restoration; limit restoration (less precise) |
| uninsured losses | Losses falling outside policy coverage due to exclusions or limits; remain insured's responsibility | uncovered losses; excluded losses (imprecise) |

## Coverage Basis — Critical Distinction

| Correct English term | Usage / meaning | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| occurrence-based coverage / loss occurrence basis | Policy covers losses occurring during policy period, regardless of when claim is filed; protects tail risk; standard for property | occurrence coverage; event-based coverage (imprecise) |

## Notes

- **Loss vs. Claim**: Many source languages use a single term for both the event and the demand for indemnity. In English, always distinguish: "loss" = the event/damage; "claim" = the legal demand for indemnity. Translate contextually based on whether the source refers to the event or the demand.

- **Occurrence vs. Claims-Made**: These are fundamentally different coverage bases. Occurrence covers events during the policy period; claims-made covers claims filed during the policy period. Always specify which applies; premium, tail coverage availability, and tail risk exposure differ dramatically.

- **Excess (UK) vs. Deductible (US)**: Equivalent terms for the same concept (insured's first-loss retention). Use UK term for UK/EU context; US term for US context. In formal EU English, prefer "excess" or both terms together ("excess/deductible") on first mention.

- **Deductible/Excess vs. Co-insurance**: Many source languages distinguish between a fixed-amount retention (deductible/excess) and a percentage retention (co-insurance/proportional retention). Ensure the correct English term is used based on whether the retention is a fixed amount or a percentage of the claim.

- **Underwriting**: Distinct from "underwriter" (person making underwriting decisions). "Underwriting" = the process of risk evaluation and pricing; "underwriter" = the professional performing that function. Both distinct from the insurance company ("insurer").

- **Lloyd's Terminology**: If document references Lloyd's market, use Lloyd's-specific terms: "slip" (not "placing document"), "underwriter" (not "insurer" in this context), "managing agent", "syndicate", "Names", "bureau". Lloyd's is not just an insurance company; it's a market of independent underwriting syndicates.

- **W&I Insurance**: Specialized product in M&A/deal contexts. Cross-reference M&A lexicon for detailed representations and warranties terminology. W&I covers breach of seller representations/warranties post-close, protecting buyer.

- **Material Non-Disclosure / Duty of Utmost Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei)**: These are foundational insurance principles. Non-disclosure of material facts may result in rescission (contract voided ab initio). This is stricter than general contract law.

- **Statute of Limitations / Prescription**: Time limit varies by jurisdiction and claim type. In UK common law: typically 6 years for contract claims, 3 years for personal injury. In EU civil law jurisdictions ("prescription"): typically 3 years. Always specify jurisdiction.

- **National Insurance Legislation**: When translating references to national insurance codes or regulatory frameworks, provide the official English translation of the statute name on first mention, followed by the original-language name in parentheses. Sub-lexicon provides source-language mappings.

- **National Insurance Supervisory Authorities**: Keep national regulatory acronyms (e.g., PRA/FCA in UK; BaFin in Germany; ACPR in France) and explain on first mention. Sub-lexicon provides source-language mappings.
