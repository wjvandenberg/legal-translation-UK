# Taxes Lexicon

English terminology for tax law, tax procedure, tax litigation, and the tax aspects
of corporate transactions. Tax is heavily jurisdiction-specific: this main lexicon
gives the neutral English vocabulary; the per-language sub-lexicons map jurisdiction-
specific tax names (IRES, IS, KSt, IRC, 法人税, 企业所得税, etc.) onto these terms
and flag where a calque would be misleading.

## Scope and cross-references

This lexicon covers:

- Categories of tax (direct, indirect, withholding, payroll, transactional)
- Tax base, deductions, credits, exemptions, rates
- Tax residency, source rules and permanent establishment
- Transfer pricing and intra-group transactions
- Double taxation conventions and relief mechanisms
- Tax compliance: returns, filings, payments, refunds
- Tax authorities, procedure, audits, rulings, settlements
- Tax litigation and appeals
- Anti-avoidance rules and tax planning concepts
- M&A and reorganisation tax (interface — full M&A drafting terms in
  `corporate-ma-jv.md`)
- Indirect tax in commercial contracts (VAT, customs, excise)
- Personal taxation, inheritance, gift, wealth tax
- Tax incentives, R&D credits, free zones

For tax warranties, tax indemnities and tax covenant drafting in M&A documents, see
the **Tax-related M&A Terms** section of `corporate-ma-jv.md` and use this lexicon
for the substantive tax vocabulary.

## Standard Verbal Anchors

These are standard English formulations used in tax documents and tax clauses:

- "for the avoidance of doubt, all amounts payable under this Agreement are exclusive
  of VAT" (price clauses)
- "the Buyer shall pay any VAT properly chargeable, against a valid tax invoice"
- "all sums payable shall be paid free and clear of any deduction or withholding,
  except as required by law" (gross-up)
- "if any deduction or withholding is required by law, the payer shall pay such
  additional amount as is necessary to ensure that the recipient receives the same
  net amount it would have received in the absence of the withholding"
- "the Seller shall indemnify the Buyer on a pound-for-pound basis against any tax
  liability of the Target arising in respect of any pre-closing tax period"
- "the parties shall co-operate in good faith to mitigate any tax leakage"
- "the parties shall not take any action which would prejudice the application of
  any tax exemption or treaty relief"
- "the parties intend the transaction to qualify for tax-neutral treatment under
  Article [●] of [●]"

## Notes

- **Translate the function, not the local label.** Use the neutral English term
  (corporate income tax, withholding tax, VAT, real estate transfer tax) and let
  the per-language sub-lexicon supply the local name. Where a tax has no real
  English equivalent (e.g., Italian IRAP — a regional production tax — or German
  Gewerbesteuer — a municipal trade tax), translate functionally and add a
  translator's note giving the original name.

- **Do not equate VAT, GST and sales tax.** They are different systems. EU/UK
  documents use VAT; Australia/India/Canada/Singapore use GST; US use sales tax.
  Translating a Spanish "IVA" or French "TVA" as "GST" or "sales tax" is wrong.

- **"Withholding tax" vs "retention tax".** "Retention tax" is a calque from
  Romance languages (ritenuta, retención, retenue) and is incorrect English. Use
  "withholding tax" (or "tax withheld at source" in narrative passages).

- **"Stamp duty" vs "registration tax".** UK "stamp duty" is conceptually similar
  to civil-law "registration tax" (registro, droit d'enregistrement, derecho de
  registro), but the technical operation differs. Use the UK term cautiously and
  add the local name on first mention.

- **"Tax authority" / "revenue authority"**: Both are acceptable. Where the source
  document refers to a named authority (Agenzia delle Entrate, Finanzamt, Hacienda,
  HMRC, IRS, 国税庁), keep the local name on first mention with a parenthetical
  English gloss.

- **"Statute of limitations" vs "prescription"**: In civil-law traditions the term
  is *prescrizione / prescription / Verjährung*, which translates literally as
  "prescription". In English tax usage, the term is "statute of limitations" or
  "limitation period". Only use "prescription" if the source materially relies on
  the civil-law mechanism and you note this.

- **Beneficial ownership**: A treaty concept that is widely abused as a literal
  translation of *beneficiario effettivo / bénéficiaire effectif / wirtschaftlich
  Berechtigter*. Distinguish between (i) beneficial ownership for treaty/WHT
  purposes (right to income), and (ii) ultimate beneficial owner (UBO) for AML
  purposes (control of the entity). Both translate to similar English but mean
  different things — keep the context clear.

- **Gross-up clauses**: When translating contractual gross-up wording, keep the
  English standard formulation. Civil-law contracts often phrase the obligation as
  a duty to "integrate" (integrare) the amount; translate functionally as
  "gross up" rather than "integrate".

- **VAT terminology is a minefield**: input/output VAT, reverse charge, place of
  supply, chargeable event and tax point have specific EU-VAT meanings. Do not
  paraphrase. If the source uses the EU concept (e.g., *operazione imponibile*,
  *exigibilité de la TVA*, *Steuerbarkeit*), use the EU-aligned English term.

- **Cryptocurrency, digital services and Pillar Two**: This area moves quickly.
  Keep the OECD-standard terminology (GloBE rules, IIR, UTPR, QDMTT) where the
  source uses it, and add a translator's note when the local-language acronym
  diverges from the OECD acronym.

- **Quantitative figures**: Tax rates, thresholds and deadlines change frequently.
  When the source cites a specific rate or threshold, reproduce it verbatim and
  do not "update" or "correct" it. If you flag a rate as out of date, do so in a
  translator's note, not in the body of the translation.

- **Currency and number formats**: Italian/German/French use the comma as decimal
  separator and the dot as thousands separator (1.234,56). UK English uses the
  reverse (1,234.56). When translating tax figures, convert the format to UK
  English style unless the user specifies otherwise.
