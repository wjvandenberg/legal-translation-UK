# IP, Technology and IT Lexicon

Standard English terminology for intellectual property and information technology agreements. Part 1 covers patents, trade marks, copyright, designs, trade secrets, licensing and technology transfer. Part 2 covers software licensing, SaaS, cloud services, data processing, development agreements and related IT instruments.

## Notes

- **"Intellectual property" scope**: Covers patents, trade marks, copyright, designs, trade secrets, and other IP rights. Many civil law jurisdictions historically separate industrial property (patents, marks, designs) from author's rights (copyright), but English uses the single umbrella term "intellectual property" for all.

- **"Trade mark" (UK) vs "trademark" (US)**: UK English (and EUIPO) use "trade mark" (two words); US uses "trademark" (one word). Default to UK primary with US alternative noted.

- **"Licence" (UK noun) vs "license" (US noun/verb)**: UK: "licence" (noun) and "license" (verb); US: "license" for both. Default to UK noun form.

- **"FRAND" terms**: Fair, Reasonable and Non-Discriminatory — standard licensing obligation for standard-essential patents (SEPs) to prevent patent holdup.

- **"SEP" (Standard-Essential Patent)**: Patent deemed necessary to practice technical standard (e.g., telecommunications, automotive); subject to FRAND licensing obligations.

- **"Freedom to operate" (FTO)**: Clearance to use technology without infringing third-party IP; requires FTO analysis covering relevant patent landscape.

- **"Prior art search"**: Comprehensive search to identify patents and publications predating invention; essential for novelty assessment and patentability determination.

- **"Patent family"**: Group of related patents protecting same invention across jurisdictions (e.g., US patent + European patent + Japanese patent); managed as single IP asset.

- **"Trade dress"**: Distinctive visual appearance of product or packaging (shape, colors, design) protected like trade mark; distinct from design registration; concept primarily used in US law; UK equivalent may reference "unregistered trade mark" via common law "passing off".

- **"Passing off" (UK)**: Common law tort in UK (and some other jurisdictions) protecting unregistered marks/get-ups by preventing third parties from using confusing similar mark causing consumer deception; US equivalent is "misappropriation" or "false passing off".

- **Capitalisation**: In legal drafting, defined terms like "Licence", "Patent", "Trade Mark", "Licensor", "Licensee" should be capitalized consistently throughout agreement.

- **Abbreviations**: Expand on first use: EPO (European Patent Office), EUIPO (European Union Intellectual Property Office), WIPO (World Intellectual Property Organization).

## Notes

- **"Licence" (UK noun) vs "license" (US noun/verb)**: UK: "licence" (noun) and "license" (verb); US: "license" for both. Default to UK noun form in legal lexicon.

- **"EULA" (End-User Licence Agreement)**: Standard software licensing contract for end-users; often presented as click-through agreement. Distinguish from development/enterprise agreements.

- **"Acceptable Use Policy" (AUP)**: Defines permitted and prohibited uses; often includes restrictions on reverse engineering, competitive use, illegal activities.

- **"Service credit" vs "liquidated damages"**: SaaS agreements typically use "service credits" (discount to invoice) for service failures; traditional IT outsourcing uses "liquidated damages" (contractual damages for breach). Not interchangeable.

- **"Service level agreement" (SLA) structure**: Should define metrics (uptime %), measurement period, service credits for failures, and exclusions (scheduled maintenance, force majeure, customer actions).

- **"Staging environment"**: Also called "test environment", "pre-production", "development environment"; used for testing before production deployment. Distinct from production.

- **"Data migration"**: Typically required at contract start (on-boarding) and end (off-boarding); agreement should specify format, timeline, security during migration.

- **"Data portability"**: Related to GDPR requirement; customer should be able to extract data in machine-readable format within reasonable timeframe.

- **"Backup and recovery"** vs **"disaster recovery"** vs **"business continuity"**: Backup = data copies; DR = restoration capability after major failure; Business continuity = broader operational resilience.

- **"Indemnity" (IP infringement)**: Developer indemnifies customer against third-party claims that developed software infringes IP rights; standard provision in development agreements.

- **"Force majeure"**: Contractual relief for unforeseeable events (war, natural disaster, pandemics) beyond parties' control; commonly excludes economic hardship.

- **"Change of control"**: Merger, acquisition, or ownership change; often triggers re-negotiation rights or termination rights due to confidentiality/security concerns.

- **"Right to audit"**: Customer's right to inspect service provider's operations to verify SLA compliance, security standards, data handling, etc.; typically subject to notice and confidentiality.

- **"Work made for hire"**: Legal concept where commissioned creator's work ownership vests with hirer (not the creator); varies by jurisdiction; should be explicitly stated in development agreements.

- **Capitalisation**: In legal drafting, defined terms like "Services", "Software", "Customer Data", "Confidential Information", "SLA", "Deliverables" should be capitalized consistently throughout agreement.

- **Service credits vs. liquidated damages in translation**: Some source languages use a single term for both concepts. In English, "service credits" (SaaS/cloud standard) and "liquidated damages" (traditional outsourcing) are distinct; always identify which applies based on context.
